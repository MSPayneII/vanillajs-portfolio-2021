Client
Tobii

Role
UX Researcher

Category
Research & Design

Team Size
2

Skills
Eye-tracking, Protocol Creation, Participant Recruitment, Survey Design, Usability Test, Data Analysis,

TL;DR

Our team piloted a two-week eye-tracking certification course hosted by Tobii. We created and administered a study to gauge the navigability of the Ross School of Business Behavioral Lab website. Then compared it to the business school websites of Columbia Business School and Stanford Graduate School of Business.

Research data determined the Ross School of Business website was the most difficult to navigate of all three websites. Also, the Ross School of Business website took the longest time to navigate, with most participants reporting moderate confusion. Recommendation: The Ross School of Business should collaborate with its web developers to make its website more user-friendly and reduce user frustration.

Project Background

I served as lab coordinator for the Ross School of Business Behavioral lab. During this time, the lab purchased a Tobii eye-tracking system, specifically the Tobii x2-60 eye-tracker model. At the time, Tobii started a new eye tracker certification and asked our team to pilot the program. The task was to design a study, administer it, and report our findings along with our feedback on the program.

Around the same time, the Ross School of Business had undergone a website redesign. Therefore we chose to build a study to gauge the effort needed to find our behavioral lab website (now discontinued).

Problem Statement

The website redesign took place without usability testing or departmental input. The lack of research lead to inaccessibility for various portions of the website from website users and staff.

The Objective

To determine user flow navigability level of the Ross School of Business Behavioral Lab website and compare it to other business school websites (Columbia Business School and Stanford Graduate School of Business).

    Research Question

  • In terms of web page navigation effort level, where does the Ross School of Business Behavioral Lab stand compared to Columbia and Stanford Behavioral lab web pages?

Process

Design

Due to having a short project window (2 weeks) and the time of the year (mid-December), we chose a within-subject design so each participant could see all three behavioral lab websites as Stimuli. The website presentation was randomized for each participant.

We collected six measures:

  • Visual Attention
  • The final number of fixation sequence
  • Brow Forrow
  • Heatmaps
  • Area of interest markers
  • Self-reporting via surveys

Seven participants took part in the study, with each receiving five short surveys: one pre-survey, three post-stimuli surveys, and a final post-survey.

Findings

Visual Attention and Facial Analysis (Brow Forrow) Results

  • On average, participants had the most fixation points on the Ross website, while participants had the least number of fixation points when navigating through the Stanford website.
  • Participants furrowed their brow more when navigating through the Ross website than they did when viewing the other websites.

Survey Results

The survey results indicate participants found that Columbia and Ross websites the most difficult to navigate through. In contrast, Stanford's behavioral lab website was consistently rated easier to navigate. Also, Stanford had the highest number of ratings of "not confused at all." Ross had the highest number of moderately confused participants.

Limitations

The study took place in the second half of December which is when our participant base is reduced due to University of Michigan staff and students going on Winterbreak. Because of this, we were only able to get seven participants. Also, navigation cueing was also a limitation but unavoidable given the circumstances.

Personal Takeaway

I learned the value of eye-tracking and how biometrics in research adds another layer of data collection that cannot be captured when using only computer surveys. With surveys, participants may not provide genuine answers for several reasons. Biometrics gives researchers the ability to record subconscious, split-second reactions to stimuli that can go unreported in self-reports.
